MedPodGPT: A Multilingual Audio-Augmented Large Language Model for Medical Research and Education

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.07.11.24310304

Abstract:

The proliferation of medical podcasts has generated an extensive repository of audio content, rich in specialized terminology, diverse medical topics, and expert dialogues. MedPodGPT is a computational framework designed to enhance large language models (LLMs) by leveraging the informational content of publicly accessible medical podcast data. This dataset, comprising over 4,300 hours of audio content, was transcribed to generate over 39 million text tokens. Our model integrates the varied dialogue found in medical podcasts to improve understanding of natural language nuances, cultural contexts, and medical knowledge. MedPodGPT demonstrated an average improvement of 2.31% over standard open-source benchmarks and showcased an improvement of 2.58% in its zero-shot multilingual transfer ability.

My Role:

I was responsible for determining and preparing the medical training datasets required for the model, focusing on curating and processing various medical podcast datasets. In addition to dataset preparation, I actively participated in the model's training process and contributed to the evaluation of its accuracy and performance across multiple benchmarks.

The Impact of Economic Factors on Native Obesity Rates: Multivariate Linear Regression Analysis

Description:

This project analyzed the impact of economic factors on obesity rates across 100 countries using multivariate linear regression. The analysis involved formulating hypotheses related to food imports, GDP, CPI, and minimum calorie requirements, and interpreting the complex relationships between these economic factors and obesity rates.

My Role:

I led the team in conducting the statistical analysis using JMP software, managed large datasets, and spearheaded the variable selection process using stepwise regression. I collaborated on data cleaning, handled outliers, and tested hypotheses, ultimately building a model with an adjusted RÂ² of 0.732431, demonstrating that 73.2% of the variability in obesity rates could be predicted from the independent variables.
